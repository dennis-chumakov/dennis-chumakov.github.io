Overview

This dissertation examined a question at the intersection of workplace rights and digital privacy: how do organisations monitor employees’ personal social media activity, and how do employees perceive the fairness and legitimacy of these practices?

Primary research was conducted via an original survey distributed across Ireland, Canada, and internationally. After filtering for employment eligibility, 64 valid responses were collected and analysed using Python.

NoteResearch Question

How do organisations monitor and respond to employees’ personal social media activity, and how do employees perceive the fairness and legitimacy of these organisational practices?

Research Journey

The dissertation was completed across four sequential components:

Component Description
1 - Research Proposal Defined the research question, objectives, and justification for primary research
2 - Literature Review Reviewed existing academic literature on workplace monitoring, privacy, and legitimacy
3 - Research Methodology Designed the survey instrument, ethical framework, and data collection approach
4 - Research Results Collected and analysed 64 survey responses using Python

Methodology

The survey instrument included:

  • Demographic questions - sector, tenure, management level
  • Policy awareness questions - whether a policy exists and is communicated
  • Likert-scale questions (Q9-Q14) - perceptions of monitoring, discipline, and behavioural impact
  • Scenario questions (Q12-Q13) - comparing appropriateness of discipline across two real-world scenarios
  • Open-ended questions (Q16-Q18) - qualitative themes around fairness, intervention, and improvement

A Wilcoxon signed-rank test was used to assess whether the difference in disciplinary appropriateness between the two scenarios was statistically significant.1

TipKey Finding

Respondents strongly agreed that monitoring creates a culture of mistrust (mean 3.97) and that monitoring constitutes an invasion of privacy (mean 3.97), while showing moderate agreement that employers should only intervene when organisational impact is direct (mean 4.05).

Scenario Comparison - When is Discipline Appropriate?

Show scenario comparison code 
stat, p_value = wilcoxon(df['q12_scenario1_num'], df['q13_scenario2_num'])

scenario_labels = ['Scenario 1 - Political Opinion',
                   'Scenario 2 - Offensive/Viral Post']

fig5.update_layout(title='Disciplinary Appropriateness by Scenario (Q12 vs Q13)',
                   yaxis=dict(range=[0, 5.5], title='Mean Appropriateness (1-5)',

                   plot_bgcolor='white')

Comparison of disciplinary appropriateness scores across two scenarios - Wilcoxon signed-rank test confirms statistically significant difference (p < 0.0001)

ImportantStatistical Finding

The Wilcoxon signed-rank test confirmed a statistically significant difference between the two scenarios (p < 0.0001). Respondents rated discipline for an offensive/viral post (Scenario 2, mean 3.97) as significantly more appropriate than for a political opinion post (Scenario 1, mean 2.40) - suggesting that context and content type are central to perceptions of legitimacy.

Reflections

This project was my first experience conducting original primary research from design through to statistical analysis. Building the survey instrument, collecting responses across multiple countries, and then writing Python to clean, encode, and visualise the data gave me an end-to-end understanding of the research process that no secondary data project could replicate.

The Wilcoxon test result was the most surprising finding - the scale of the difference between the two scenarios (mean gap of 1.57) suggested that employees draw a very clear line between political expression and harmful/offensive content when judging disciplinary legitimacy.
